# []()[Chapter 01 – Introduction]()

## [1.1 Background and Context]()

Personal
financial management has become an essential competency within the modern
digital economy. Individuals are increasingly responsible for managing multiple
income sources, recurring expenses, digital subscriptions, and short-term
financial commitments. The widespread adoption of cashless transactions and
online payment platforms has increased convenience; however, it has also
reduced individuals’ ability to accurately monitor spending behaviour (OECD, 2019). As a result,
limited financial visibility has often led to ineffective budgeting practices
and increased financial stress.

Despite
the availability of numerous digital finance applications, many individuals
continue to rely on manual financial tracking methods, including notebooks and
spreadsheets. These approaches are time-consuming, prone to human error, and
provide limited analytical insight into spending patterns. Existing digital
solutions frequently prioritise advanced automation or bank integrations rather
than usability, which has resulted in reduced long-term adoption due to
complexity (Laudon & Laudon, 2020).

The Smart
Financial Tracker (SFT) project proposes a web-based personal finance tracking
system that prioritises simplicity, clarity, and user awareness. The system
enables users to manually record income and expense transactions, monitor
weekly and monthly budgets, and visualise financial information through
dashboards and charts. The project is developed as part of an academic
requirement and demonstrates the practical application of computer science
principles to address a real-world financial management problem.

## [1.2 Problem Statement]()

Personal
financial management continues to be a challenge for many individuals due to
limited visibility and control over daily financial activities. The key
problems identified are as follows:

·
Many
individuals lack a structured and consistent method for recording income and
expenses, resulting in incomplete or inaccurate financial records.

·
Manual
tracking methods such as notebooks and spreadsheets are time-consuming, prone
to human error, and difficult to maintain over long periods.

·
Users
often fail to recognise spending patterns early due to the absence of real-time
summaries and visual feedback.

·
Existing
digital finance applications frequently prioritise automation and complex
features, which discourages continued usage by non-technical users.

·
The lack
of timely financial insight contributes to overspending, ineffective budgeting,
and delayed financial decision-making.

·
The Smart
Financial Tracker aims to address these issues by providing a simple,
structured, and insight-driven platform for personal finance monitoring.

## [1.3 Scope and limitations]()

### [Project Scope]()

•
**The system allows users to manually record income and expense
transactions in a structured manner.**

This approach ensures consistent and accurate data entry while encouraging
users to actively engage with their financial activities. Manual recording also
improves awareness of spending habits and reduces reliance on automated
processes that may obscure financial behaviour.

•
**Users can categorise financial records to better organise and analyse
spending behaviour.**

Categorisation supports clearer interpretation of financial data by grouping
similar expenses and income sources. This enables users to compare spending
across categories and identify areas where financial adjustments may be
required.

•
**Weekly and monthly budgets can be set and monitored to support spending
control.**

Supporting both weekly and monthly budgeting allows users to manage finances
across different time horizons. This structure helps users detect overspending
early and adjust behaviour before long-term financial imbalance occurs.

•
**Real-time financial summaries and visual dashboards provide clear
insight into income, expenses, and balances.**

Visual dashboards present financial information in an intuitive format that
supports quick understanding and pattern recognition. Real-time updates ensure
that users base decisions on the most current financial data available.

•
**Secure user authentication ensures that financial data is stored and
accessed safely.**

Authentication mechanisms restrict access to authorised users only, protecting
sensitive personal financial information. This also increases user trust in the
system and supports responsible data management practices.

### [Project Limitations]()

·
The system
does not integrate with banks or external financial services for automatic
transaction imports.

·
Advanced
financial features such as investments, loans, and credit management are not
included.

·
The system
stores financial records using a single base currency; however, currency
conversion may be provided as a reference feature without supporting
multi-currency transaction storage.

·
The system
is designed for individual users and does not support organisational or
multi-user finance management.

·
These
limitations are applied to maintain simplicity and ensure completion within the
academic timeframe.

The Smart

enabling users to clearly monitor income, expenses, and budgeting performance
through visual and structured feedback.

   / Plymouth University) – Oversees assessment quality and learning outcomes.**

requirements and assessment regulations. It also evaluates the project’s

5. **Future Developers or
   Maintainers – Potential contributors responsible for system enhancement or
   maintenance.**

Future developers may extend or maintain the system after project completion.
Clear documentation and modular design support maintainability and future
feature enhancements.

# [Chapter 04 – Literature Review]()

## [**4.1 Personal Financial Management

Systems**]()

Personal Financial Management (PFM) systems are widely
recognised as digital tools that support individuals in monitoring income,
expenses, and savings while improving overall financial awareness. Research
demonstrates that structured financial tracking contributes positively to
budgeting discipline and financial capability by encouraging consistent
engagement with personal financial data (Xiao & O’Neill, 2018). Such engagement has
been shown to improve users’ understanding of spending patterns and financial
outcomes over time.

Despite these benefits, sustained adoption of PFM systems
remains inconsistent. Evidence indicates that long-term usage is strongly
influenced by system usability rather than the availability of advanced
features. Studies examining user behaviour reveal that applications perceived
as complex are frequently abandoned, even when they offer sophisticated
analytical capabilities (Kim, et al., 2003). This highlights a
limitation in systems that prioritise functionality without sufficient
consideration of user experience.

In recent financial technologies, automation—particularly
through bank integrations and transaction synchronisation—has become
increasingly prominent. While automation reduces manual data entry and improves
data accuracy, it also introduces additional layers of complexity and
dependency on external services (Laudon & Laudon, 2020). These
characteristics may present barriers for users with limited technical literacy
and raise concerns related to transparency and data privacy.

Conversely, evidence from behavioural finance literature
indicates that manual or semi-manual transaction recording can enhance
financial awareness. Active involvement in entering financial data has been
associated with greater cognitive engagement and stronger accountability for
spending decisions (Baker & Yannelis, 2017). However, such
systems receive limited attention in contemporary fintech research, which
continues to favour automation-driven approaches (Xiao &
 O’Neill, 2018).

Taken together, existing literature reveals an imbalance
between efficiency-oriented automation and user-centred financial awareness.
While automated systems dominate both research and practice, simplified
financial tracking tools that emphasise behavioural engagement and long-term
usability remain underexplored.

## [**4.2 Budgeting and Financial

Awareness**]()

Budgeting is consistently recognised as a central mechanism
in effective personal financial management. Empirical research demonstrates
that individuals who actively monitor budgets are more likely to regulate
discretionary spending, reduce financial stress, and improve overall financial
stability (OECD, 2019).Budgeting practices
that encourage regular review and reflection have been shown to support more
informed financial decision-making.

Despite this, the effectiveness of budgeting tools is
strongly influenced by how financial information is presented and updated.
Evidence suggests that budget awareness is significantly enhanced when users
receive timely and ongoing feedback regarding spending progress rather than
retrospective summaries (OECD, 2019).
Systems that rely solely on end-of-period reports often fail to support
behavioural adjustment during the budgeting cycle.

A further limitation identified in existing applications is
the predominant focus on long-term or monthly budgeting structures. Research
indicates that many users make frequent, short-term spending decisions that
require immediate feedback to support behavioural regulation (Xiao & O’Neill, 2018)Budgeting tools that
do not accommodate shorter budgeting intervals may therefore offer limited
practical value for everyday financial management.

Behavioural finance literature further highlights that
awareness alone is insufficient to influence financial behaviour. The
integration of behavioural reinforcement mechanisms, such as progress
indicators and reminders, has been shown to support behavioural adjustment and
sustained engagement (Thaler & Sunstein, 2008).However, many
existing financial applications provide limited behavioural feedback, reducing
their capacity to translate budgeting information into meaningful action.

Overall, the literature suggests a gap between established
budgeting theory and its practical implementation in digital financial systems,
particularly in relation to short-term feedback and behavioural reinforcement.

## [**4.3 Data Visualisation in

Financial Applications**]()

Data visualisation is widely acknowledged as a critical
component of effective financial information systems. Visual representations
such as charts and graphs support users in interpreting complex financial data
by reducing cognitive effort and enabling pattern recognition (Few, 2013). In personal finance
contexts, well-designed visualisations allow users to quickly understand
spending distributions, income trends, and budget performance.

Research emphasises that effective financial visualisation
prioritises clarity and relevance over visual complexity. Studies on
information design demonstrate that minimal, purpose-driven dashboards enhance
comprehension and decision-making by focusing attention on key metrics (Tufte, 2001).Visual elements that
directly support user goals are more likely to facilitate meaningful insight
than dense or decorative interfaces.

However, not all financial applications adhere to these
principles. Evidence indicates that excessive visual elements, poor visual
hierarchy, and information overload can overwhelm users and obscure critical
insights (Few, 2013) (Ware, 2013). Dashboards that
prioritise aesthetics over analytical clarity may reduce usability and
discourage sustained engagement.

In many commercial financial systems, data visualisation is
treated as an optional enhancement rather than a core analytical tool. This
approach contrasts with established research, which positions visualisation as
a fundamental mechanism for supporting understanding and behavioural awareness.
The limited application of evidence-based visual design principles highlights
an underutilisation of visualisation as a behavioural support feature in
personal finance applications.

## [**4.4 Identified Research, Technological,

and Methodological Gaps**]()

A synthesis of existing literature reveals several
interconnected gaps that constrain the effectiveness of current personal
financial management solutions. These gaps span behavioural support,
technological design, and development methodology, and collectively inform the
rationale for this project.

### [**Research Gap: Limited Support

for Behavioural Financial Change**]()

While many financial systems effectively record and
summarise transactions, they offer limited support for behavioural reflection
and habit formation. Research indicates that financial awareness does not
consistently translate into behavioural change unless users are encouraged to
understand the underlying causes of their spending patterns (Baker & Yannelis, 2017).Most systems present
historical data without sufficient contextual interpretation, reducing their
influence on future decision-making.

Additionally, behavioural studies highlight the importance
of reinforcement mechanisms in shaping financial behaviour. Systems that lack
behavioural cues, such as reminders or progress indicators, often fail to
convert financial information into actionable insight (Thaler &
 Sunstein, 2008).
This reveals a gap in solutions that actively support behavioural improvement
rather than passive monitoring.

### [**Technological Gap: Fragmented

Architectures and Delayed Feedback**]()

From a technological perspective, many personal finance
systems rely on fragmented architectures that separate data processing from
presentation layers. Such designs frequently result in delayed updates and
reduced system responsiveness, limiting real-time financial awareness (Laudon & Laudon, 2020).Applications that
depend on periodic data synchronisation further restrict the availability of
immediate financial feedback, particularly for short-term budgeting and expense
monitoring.

There is limited evidence of lightweight, unified
architectures that prioritise real-time feedback while maintaining simplicity
and maintainability. This technological gap restricts the ability of existing
systems to provide timely, insight-driven financial information.

### [**Methodological Gap: Insufficient

User-Centred Development Practices**]()

The literature also highlights methodological limitations
in the development of financial software systems. Rigid development approaches
often prioritise technical completeness over usability, resulting in systems
that fail to align with real-world user needs (Sommerville, 2016). Limited user
involvement during development contributes to reduced adoption and early
abandonment.

There is a lack of documented approaches that integrate
iterative development practices with user-centred financial design. This
methodological gap underscores the need for development processes that
continuously align system functionality with user behaviour and expectations.

# [Chapter 05 Method of Approach]()

## [5.1 Research Design]()

A
prototype-based development approach grounded in applied computer science
practice is adopted for the Smart Financial Tracker project. This approach
supports iterative refinement of system features, interface design, and
business logic through continuous testing and evaluation.

## [5.2 Data Sources and Data Handling]()

User-generated
financial data serve as the primary data source. Transactions are manually
entered and securely stored in a database associated with authenticated user
accounts. External financial services are not integrated, reducing data privacy
risks.

## [5.3 Tools, Technologies, and Frameworks]()

The system
is developed using a full-stack technology stack. The frontend is implemented
using React, while the backend is developed using Node.js and Express. MongoDB
is used for data storage. Git and GitHub support version control and source
code management.

## [5.4 System Logic and Processing]()

The system
focuses on deterministic financial calculations, including income totals,
expense totals, balances, budget usage, and time-based comparisons.
Calculations are performed dynamically to ensure immediate updates.

## [5.5 Evaluation and Validation Strategy]()

Functional
validation and manual testing are used to verify data accuracy, system
stability, and budget logic. System behaviour is evaluated across multiple
usage scenarios.

## [5.6 Project Management Methodology]()

An
Agile-inspired iterative development approach is applied. Development tasks are
organised into manageable iterations, supported by frequent testing and regular
version control commits.

# [Chapter 08 – Risk Analysis]()

Budget constraints.

As the
project is developed within an academic context, financial resources are
limited and rely mainly on free or open-source tools, which may restrict
advanced deployment options. This risk is minimised by using open-source
technologies and free-tier cloud services.

Technical
complexity of the MERN stack.

Integrating
frontend, backend, and database components may introduce development challenges
and integration errors. This risk is managed through modular design, clear API
structures, and incremental testing.

Performance
issues in real-time data visualisation.

Frequent
updates to dashboards and charts may reduce system responsiveness if not
optimised. Efficient database queries and controlled UI re-rendering will be
applied to maintain acceptable performance.

Errors in
financial calculations.

Incorrect
income or expense calculations could affect data accuracy and user trust.
Validation logic and thorough testing across different financial scenarios will
be used to reduce this risk.

Data security
and privacy risks.

The system
stores sensitive personal financial information, making it vulnerable to
unauthorised access if security is insufficient. Secure authentication,
encrypted password storage, and access controls will be implemented to protect
user data.

Data loss
or system failure.

Unexpected
system or database failures could result in loss of financial records. Reliable
data handling practices and error-handling mechanisms will be used to ensure
data integrity.

Limited
development time.

Academic
deadlines and other coursework may limit the available time for development and
testing. This risk is mitigated by prioritising core features and following an
iterative development approach.

Scope
creep during development.

Adding
unplanned features may delay project completion and reduce overall quality.
Strict adherence to the defined project scope and regular progress reviews will
prevent unnecessary expansion.

Usability
challenges.

If the
interface is not intuitive, users may find the system difficult to use and
discontinue usage. User-centred design principles and iterative interface
improvements will be applied to enhance usability.
