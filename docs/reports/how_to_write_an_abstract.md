**How
to Write an Abstract **

An abstract is a standalone,
concise summary of a research project, report, or study. Its purpose is to
communicate the most important elements of the work—what was done, why it was
done, how it was done, what was found, and why it matters—within a very limited
word count. Because the abstract is often the most read section of an academic
document, it must be clear, accurate, and informative.

An abstract should always be
written after the main document is completed, as it must faithfully represent
the final content of the study.

# Purpose of an Abstract

The abstract serves multiple
audiences, including:

•
Examiners and supervisors

•
Journal editors and reviewers

•
Conference reviewers and attendees

•
Researchers conducting literature reviews

•
Policy makers or practitioners (in some cases)

A well-written abstract:

•
Determines whether the full document will be
read

•
Influences acceptance for publication,
conferences, or funding

•
Increases the visibility and impact of the
research **Key Characteristics
of a Good Abstract **

A good abstract is:

•
Clear and concise (typically 150–300 words
unless stated otherwise)

•
Self-contained (understandable without reading
the full document)

•
Accurate and representative of the study

•
Written in formal academic language

•
Free of citations, figures, tables, and
references

•
Written objectively, usually in the past tense
for completed work

# What to Include in an Abstract

Although formats vary (structured or unstructured), most
research abstracts contain the same core elements.

1. Background
   / Introduction

Briefly explain the context
and rationale for the study. This section should:

•
Identify the problem or gap in knowledge

•
Explain why the study is necessary

•
Be limited to one or two concise sentences

Avoid extensive background
theory or literature discussion.

2. Aim
   / Purpose

Clearly state what the study
set out to do. A strong aim should specify:

•
The focus of the study

•
The population or context (if relevant)

•
The key concept or outcome of interest

The aim should logically
follow from the background and address the identified problem.

3. Methods

Summarise how the study was
conducted. This section should include:

•
Study design (e.g., experimental, survey,
qualitative, mixed methods)

•
Data collection methods

•
Sample size or data sources (where relevant)

•
Analysis approach (at a high level)

The methods must provide enough
information to judge rigor, without unnecessary detail.

4. Results
   / Findings

This is the most critical part of
the abstract. It should:

•
Present the key findings only

•
Directly address the stated aim

•
Use precise and academic language

•
Avoid vague statements such as “results were
discussed” or “several factors were identified”

Only the most important outcomes should be included.

5. Conclusion
   / Implications

Explain what the results mean.
This section should:

•
Interpret the findings in relation to the aim

•
Indicate significance for practice, research, or
policy

•
Avoid introducing new results or exaggerated
claims

Conclusions should be balanced,
realistic, and supported by the results presented.

# What NOT to Include in an Abstract

An abstract should not
contain:

•
Detailed explanations or background theory

•
Citations or reference lists

•
Figures, tables, or diagrams

•
Undefined abbreviations

•
Future work promises not supported by results

•
Personal opinions or informal language

# Step-by-Step Guide

To write an effective abstract:

Complete the full report first

Identify one or two key points from each major section

Combine them into a single, coherent summary

Edit rigorously to remove redundancy and improve
clarity

Ensure the abstract strictly follows word limits and
submission guidelines

Revision is essential. A
strong abstract typically goes through multiple drafts**.**

# Example

Abstract (Illustrative Only)

This project investigates the
limitations of manual attendance systems used in higher education institutions.
The aim of the study is to develop an automated attendance management system
using face recognition technology. The proposed solution employs a convolutional
neural network-based model trained on labeled facial images and is implemented
as a web-based application. Experimental results indicate that the system
achieves high recognition accuracy while reducing the time required to record
attendance. These findings demonstrate the potential of biometric technologies
to enhance efficiency and reliability in academic administrative processes.
